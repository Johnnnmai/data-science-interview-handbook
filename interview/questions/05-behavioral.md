# Behavioral Questions

Behavioral interviews for DS roles evaluate collaboration, communication, stakeholder management, and decision-making under uncertainty. They are not afterthoughts - at companies like Amazon, Meta, and Google, behavioral performance can override technical scores.


## How Behavioral Rounds Work

Most DS behavioral rounds are 30-45 minutes. The interviewer asks 3-5 questions using the STAR format (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Some companies map questions to their corporate values (Amazon: Leadership Principles, Google: Googleyness, Meta: Move Fast).

Prepare 6-8 strong stories from your work experience. Each story should cover a different competency. Reuse and adapt stories across questions rather than memorizing separate answers for every possible question.


## Question: Disagreement with a Stakeholder

Tell me about a time you disagreed with a product manager or business stakeholder about a data-driven recommendation.

What they evaluate: Can you advocate for your analysis without being combative? Do you listen to business context you might be missing? Do you compromise when appropriate?

How to answer: describe the specific disagreement, the data behind your position, how you communicated your analysis, what you learned from the stakeholder's perspective, and the outcome. The best answers show mutual learning - you influenced the decision AND you adjusted your approach based on their input.


## Question: Communicating Uncertainty

Describe a time you had to present uncertain or inconclusive results to leadership.

What they evaluate: Do you hide behind p-values or communicate actionable insight despite uncertainty? Can you quantify what you do not know?

How to answer: describe the analysis, why the results were uncertain (small sample, confounding factors, ambiguous signal), how you framed the uncertainty for a non-technical audience, what recommendation you made despite the uncertainty, and what happened next.


## Question: Prioritization Under Ambiguity

You have three analysis requests from three different teams, all marked as urgent. How do you prioritize?

What they evaluate: Can you assess business impact, not just urgency? Do you communicate proactively about tradeoffs?

How to answer: describe your prioritization framework (business impact, time sensitivity, effort required, reversibility of the decision being informed). Explain how you communicate with stakeholders about timeline expectations. Show that you can say no or negotiate scope without damaging relationships.


## Question: A Project That Failed

Tell me about a data science project that did not deliver the expected results.

What they evaluate: Do you learn from failure? Can you identify what went wrong without blaming others? Do you adjust your approach?

How to answer: describe the project, what you expected, what actually happened, the root cause of the failure (data quality, wrong assumptions, scope creep, misaligned expectations), what you learned, and how you applied that lesson to subsequent projects. Avoid stories where someone else was entirely at fault - interviewers want to see self-awareness.


## Question: Cross-Functional Collaboration

Describe a project where you worked closely with engineers or product managers. What was your role and how did you ensure alignment?

What they evaluate: Can you work effectively across functions? Do you understand the constraints and priorities of other roles?

How to answer: describe the project structure, your specific contributions, how you communicated with non-DS team members, how you handled disagreements or misaligned priorities, and the outcome. Show that you understand what engineers and PMs care about, not just what data scientists care about.


## Question: Influencing Without Authority

Tell me about a time you convinced a team to adopt a data-driven approach when they were skeptical.

What they evaluate: Can you influence decisions without positional power? Do you build trust through evidence?

How to answer: describe the initial skepticism, how you understood their concerns, what evidence you presented, how you iterated based on feedback, and the outcome. The best answers show patience and relationship building, not just throwing data at people.


## Question: Handling Ambiguous Requirements

A PM comes to you and says "we need to understand our users better." How do you turn this into an actionable project?

What they evaluate: Can you take a vague request and structure it into something useful? Do you ask the right clarifying questions?

How to answer: describe the clarifying questions you would ask (what decisions will this inform? what user behaviors are you most concerned about? what data do we already have?), how you would scope the analysis, what deliverable you would propose, and how you would set expectations about timeline and depth.


## Question: Ethical Considerations

Have you ever encountered an ethical concern in a data science project? How did you handle it?

What they evaluate: Do you think about the downstream impact of your work? Can you raise concerns constructively?

How to answer: describe the situation (biased training data, privacy concerns, potentially harmful model outputs), how you identified the issue, who you raised it with, what alternatives you proposed, and the outcome. If you have not encountered this directly, describe a hypothetical scenario you have thought about and how you would approach it.


## Preparation Tips

1. Map your stories to common competencies: collaboration, communication, leadership, failure/learning, ambiguity, conflict resolution
2. Quantify results whenever possible - "increased model accuracy by 12%" is stronger than "improved the model"
3. Practice out loud - behavioral answers that sound good in your head often ramble when spoken
4. Keep answers to 2-3 minutes each - the interviewer should have time for follow-up questions
5. Prepare questions for the interviewer - ask about team structure, how DS work is prioritized, or how experiments are run
